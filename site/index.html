Reinventing the ISP

From Netflix, YouTube and Instagram to general ubiquitous metered Internet.

The Internet's next act — how a neutral federation protocol evolves Internet Service Providers from selling connectivity to clearing Internet service usage, stewarding the data commons, and distributing the applications themselves.

Now

ISPs now — connectivity companies

Internet Service Providers laid the subsea cables, pulled the fiber and raised the towers — absorbing the capital risk of building the physical Internet. Yet the outsized returns are captured one layer up, by platforms that own the user relationship and the data it produces.

01

ISPs built the Internet’s body, but the value is captured above it

The ISP was reduced to a commodity pipe — selling undifferentiated bandwidth at shrinking margins, structurally excluded from the economics of the very services that traverse its network. Every stream, every search, every transaction crosses its wires; none of that value stops there.

In depth
02

Hyperscalers rule through two moats — captive distribution and hoarded data

Walled gardens own the user relationship end to end, so a small team can’t reach the market without paying the gatekeeper’s toll. And a handful of platforms own the world’s telemetry — data is users, yet users see none of the value their data creates. Advertising funds the “free” services, which makes attention the product being sold.

In depth
03

DePIN tried to break this with tokens — and proved infrastructure can’t run on speculation

Token-incentivized networks rewarded yield-chasing instead of real demand; when prices crashed, the infrastructure collapsed with them. The overlooked fact: the Internet already is the most successful decentralized physical infrastructure network in existence, with the ISP as its building block. What’s missing is not new infrastructure — it’s a neutral protocol to unlock the assets ISPs already own.

In depth

In depth · 01

The Internet was built twice, by two very different kinds of business. One laid cables under oceans and pulled fiber through streets — slow, regulated work whose costs are recovered over decades. The other wrote software that could reach the whole world from a single datacenter, at a marginal cost near zero. When the two met, the returns flowed to the fast side, and the risk stayed with the slow one.

This was not the result of anyone’s malice. The early network was remarkably decentralized, but the products users came to expect — search, recommendations, always-available state — could not be engineered across a loose federation of machines with the tools of the time. Computation drifted into a handful of enormous buildings because that was where those problems could be solved; the data followed the computation, and the network connecting it all became a commodity — necessary, invisible, and paid accordingly.

Yet the network operator’s position never actually disappeared. Every interaction, however sophisticated the service behind it, still enters and leaves through the same last mile; the ISP remains the one party present in all of them. What operators lacked for thirty years was not relevance but a way to take part in the value passing through them — one that did not require becoming yet another platform. That missing instrument is what the rest of this story supplies.

In depth · 02

The dominance of today’s platforms rests on two advantages that reinforce each other. The first is control of the paths by which software reaches people — the stores, the defaults, the feeds, each taking its share from anyone who needs them. The second is data: a larger audience produces more behavioral signal, which produces a better and better-targeted product, which draws a larger audience still. A newcomer with superior engineering starts behind on both counts, and neither gap can be closed by engineering alone.

Advertising is what pays for this machine, and its influence runs deeper than the banners. A service that is free at the point of use must earn its keep from attention, so the product is tuned to capture as much of it as possible — and the surveillance that troubles regulators is not an excess of the model but its bookkeeping, since attention can only be sold if behavior is measured.

The least visible cost falls on shared knowledge itself. An index of the web or a map of the world is not an invention; it is a record of what humanity has collectively produced. When such records are owned by companies that finance them through advertising, what they show is shaped by what sells. And everyone who might build something better must first buy access — at the owner’s price, on the owner’s terms — to a picture of their own world.

In depth · 03

The first serious attempt to decentralize physical infrastructure came from the crypto world, and its reasoning was straightforward: if people were paid in tokens to run antennas, storage and compute nodes from their homes, a bottom-up alternative to the telecom and cloud incumbents would gradually assemble itself.

What assembled instead was a lesson in incentives. The people deploying hardware were there for the token, not the network — so supply grew whenever prices rose and vanished whenever they fell, while real demand never arrived, because no business can depend on infrastructure run by anonymous hobbyists offering no guarantees, no contracts and no one accountable when it fails. Infrastructure, it turned out, cannot be conjured from speculation; it must be carried by entities with real revenues and real obligations.

The conclusion usually drawn — that decentralized infrastructure is a dream — misses something obvious. A decentralized physical network of exactly the kind imagined already exists, and has for decades: tens of thousands of independent operators, bound together by common protocols, with paid-for equipment in the ground and billing relationships reaching nearly every household on earth. The Internet itself is proof that the model works. What its operators have never had is the equivalent, at the level of money, of what the Internet’s protocols gave them at the level of data — a neutral standard through which thousands of independent businesses can act as one.

Act I

ISPs becoming clearing companies

The Edge Settlement Protocol federates ISPs into a shared metering and clearing layer. Users keep paying the one bill they already pay; applications never invoice, token-gate or collect cards. Every request is metered at the edge and settled automatically from an aggregated clearing pool.

01

Existing connectivity bill is used to settle all Internet service usage

The connectivity bill becomes a passport to the whole catalog. Like a city multipass covering every gym and pool, users pay a modest blended premium for commitment-free access to everything they actually touch — and subscription fatigue disappears.

In depth
02

Clearing Internet service usage is a new revenue stream for ISPs

Every metered request clears through the ISP, so the operator finally shares in the economics that used to pass straight over its network. Clearing folds into core operations as a predictable new revenue line — earned from the value the network carries, not just the bandwidth it sells.

In depth
03

Metered approach to Internet service usage liberates users from subscriptions and advertisements

Because every service settles on the single connectivity bill, users escape the pile of per-app subscriptions — and because usage is paid for directly, the everyday services people rely on no longer need to run on advertising. One metered relationship replaces both.

In depth

In depth · 01

For the household, the change is deliberately invisible. The contract, the support line and the monthly invoice all remain with the same provider; nothing new has to be installed, understood or signed up for. What changes is what the invoice covers: a single recurring payment now settles everything consumed through the connection, and no service ever again asks for a card, a subscription or an account.

Underneath, every small act of consumption — a search, a minute of video, a question put to a model — is measured where it happens, at the edge of the network, and recorded as a verifiable event. The providers collectively keep a shared settlement pool funded in advance; as usage is confirmed against it, applications are paid immediately. A builder no longer faces millions of customers who must each be billed, but a single counterparty that always pays.

The arrangement is economically sound for the same reason a city pass is. Almost nobody maintains separate memberships at every gym and pool in town, yet many will pay a modest premium for one pass that opens all of them. Blanket access unlocks precisely the consumption that per-service pricing suppresses — the casual visit, the passing curiosity — and because millions of households share one pool, individual fluctuations average out. Usage is metered strictly underneath; what the household experiences is an Internet that simply works, settled by one honest bill.

In depth · 02

In every mature market, the party that stands between transacting sides and guarantees settlement is paid for it — card networks, clearing houses and exchanges all live on this role. The Internet has been the exception: an economy of billions of daily transactions running over operators who earn nothing from any of them. Reinvented as clearing companies, ISPs close that anomaly. They front the liquidity from which services are paid, verify the records of usage, absorb the reconciliation and the disputes — and earn from this financial work a revenue stream unrelated to selling bandwidth.

Federation is what makes the role valuable. Any single operator’s traffic is a fragment, of little interest to anyone beyond it; thousands of operators clearing through one pool constitute the deepest market on the Internet, and a small share of that market is worth more than complete ownership of a fragment. Joining is rational not despite the sharing, but because of it.

The role also carries influence of a kind ISPs have never held. A say in how the system evolves is distributed according to activity actually cleared — not capital, not tokens — so the operators who carry the network’s economy hold a proportionate hand on its rules. After three decades of watching the Internet’s direction being set above their heads, the companies that own its physical body acquire a vote weighted by the work they do.

In depth · 03

Everything here rests on how small the unit of payment is: a single query, half a minute of video, one answer from a model. When the unit is this fine, paying follows using exactly — and all the machinery invented to manage coarse payment loses its reason to exist. Plans, tiers, bundles and annual commitments are, at bottom, ways of selling in bulk what could not previously be sold by the piece.

The subscription deserves particular scrutiny, because its economics are quietly adversarial: it earns most from the customers who forget it exists, and the doubt it plants — will I use this enough to justify it? — kills more usage than the fee itself. Metered settlement removes the question, and with it the vendor’s stake in the customer’s inertia. It is also more honest in a literal sense: every metered event is visible to the household, itemized and verifiable, where the bundle asks to be trusted.

The consequences ripple outward into what gets built at all. A niche service loved by ten thousand people — hopeless under advertising, hopeless behind a paywall — earns a living in an economy that pays per use, so software stops needing a mass audience to deserve existence. And advertising, no longer the only way to fund a free-feeling product, must henceforth defend itself against rivals offering the same thing without it. Nothing bans the ad; what disappears is the absence of an alternative.

Act II

ISPs becoming data companies — and no, not by spying on traffic

In the legacy web, one company’s crawler reads the web, one company’s cars photograph the streets, and everyone’s behavior is siphoned in secret to sell ads. ESP inverts the model: data is collected in the open, aggregated under strict privacy guarantees, and published into a commons no one owns.

01

ISPs make up the largest, widest data ingest in the world, paving the way for the largest datasets ever created

The modern household is becoming a sensor array: the car films every street it drives, robots map the home, and daily Internet use leaves a behavioral trail. Today that value evaporates — or drains into manufacturers’ silos. The federated ISP is the one everpresent counterparty positioned to ingest it all — with consent, on the household’s behalf.

In depth
02

Ingested and aggregated data become public commons datasets, not private data silos

What the network ingests doesn’t disappear into a proprietary vault. Anonymized, aggregated datasets are published to an open commons anyone can build on — AI pipelines, researchers, competitors — dissolving the private data moat that hyperscalers guard today.

In depth
03

Data aggregation work is a new revenue stream for ISPs

Producing and refreshing those datasets is real work — and the protocol pays for it. Buyers fund the collection, not exclusive ownership, so aggregation becomes a new, recurring revenue line for ISPs that flows back as a visible discount on the household’s bill.

In depth

In depth · 01

Households are about to produce more valuable data than most companies do. A car with cameras records the streets it drives through. Every task a household robot performs, from vacuuming today to housework tomorrow, is a recorded example of real physical work — training data that robotics labs currently cannot obtain at any price. Everyday browsing adds a record of what people look for and choose. Even the broadband line itself has a use, since a crawler running from a residential connection sees the ordinary web — the local versions, the pages served to real visitors — that datacenter crawlers get blocked from or shown substitutes for. Almost none of this is collected today, and the part that is collected belongs to whichever manufacturer happened to ship the device.

No single company is in a position to gather these streams together. The car maker gets its own cars, the robot vendor its own robots, and even the largest platform sees only what happens inside its own products. An ISP already has a paid relationship with the household, a legal presence in the same country, and a wire into every one of those devices. Thousands of ISPs acting through a common protocol can therefore collect across all of it, and can do it in the open: data is gathered when a buyer commissions it, each operator handles only its own subscribers, personal detail is removed before anything is published, and the household gets a share back via its regular monthly bill. Whether the car’s cameras contribute at all, and to which network, stays the owner’s decision rather than the manufacturer’s.

What comes out is hard to obtain by any other means: street imagery refreshed by daily commutes, manipulation data from household robots at population scale, a continuously updated index of the web, behavioral aggregates covering entire countries. Building any one of these requires being present in millions of homes at once, and that is the position ISPs already occupy.

In depth · 02

The defining rule of the data market is that the buyer never takes possession of the data. A purchase funds a round of collection, and the output — anonymized and aggregated until no individual can be recovered — is published openly for anyone to read. What a buyer pays for is the data existing and being current: the slice it needs is often missing or stale until someone funds its collection. Rivals reading the same dataset afterward takes nothing away from that, and a commissioned refresh still costs far less than running a private collection network — the only other way to get the data at all.

Because the dataset is open and excludes no one, no single buyer has to fund all of it. Different organizations pay to refresh the parts they care about — a logistics firm keeping road and street imagery current, a retailer funding collection of shopping behavior, a government improving coverage of its own region, an AI lab commissioning whatever it needs to train on. Each request is placed through the same protocol and fulfilled by the same collector — the federated ISP collective — and every result accumulates in the same dataset. Each funder therefore improves the data for everyone else as a byproduct of serving itself, and the cost of keeping the commons fresh is spread across many parties with unrelated interests rather than resting on any single one.

Once these datasets are open to everyone, holding data stops being an advantage in itself, and the contest moves to what gets built on top of them. The record itself — what the web contains, what the streets look like — also stops being kept by companies whose revenue depends on which version of it people are shown.

In depth · 03

Maintaining the commons is real work — collection has to run, anonymization has to hold, quality has to be checked, storage has to be paid for — and the system treats it as work rather than charity. Every commissioned refresh routes payment to the operators who performed it, and because demand for fresh data never ends, the income arrives not as a windfall but as a recurring line of business: the second revenue stream the ISP acquires in this story.

Part of that stream flows onward to the households whose activity, in aggregate, made the data worth anything. Here the design bows to human nature. People will not run wallets to collect cents; the worth of any one person’s signal is genuinely small, and paying it out directly feels like a chore dressed up as an opportunity. Folded instead into the bill that already exists — as a visible discount, applied automatically, for everyone — the same money becomes something a household actually notices. The operator does the harvesting; the subscriber simply pays less.

In the aggregate, this is the revenue that replaces advertising as the patron of the everyday Internet. Search, mail, news and video have always been paid for by something. Financed from data and computation sold openly, rather than attention sold covertly, the free tier survives with its incentives repaired: the services need their users satisfied, not captivated.

Act III

ISPs becoming app distribution companies

In the ESP model an application is a deployable package. Founders build the product; ISPs and their datacenters run it as local franchises, close to their own subscribers. The protocol splits every cleared payment automatically — operational revenue to the operator, royalty to the founder.

01

New kind of apps break the old trade-off between convenience and control

These apps feel as fast and polished as anything the big platforms offer — yet nothing is taken in exchange. Your data stays under your control and portable: no signup walls, no forced ads, and nothing that ties you to a single provider. And the apps are natively jurisdiction-aware — each user is served by a legally accountable operator in their own country, under their own laws, without forking the product.

In depth
02

Small founder teams win — distribution, billing and compliance come with the network

Freed from the operational overhead, a compact team reaches millions and competes on product quality alone — operators everywhere deploy, market and support its app. Alongside the founders a new class of entity emerges: local Site Operators who run catalogues of apps, specialize in finance, media or AI, and carry the legal weight in each jurisdiction.

In depth
03

Monetizing routing decisions is a new revenue stream for ISPs

Because the ISP controls the routing tables, it decides which site serves each request. It can host the app on-net to capture the full operational revenue, or steer that traffic to partners who pay for the feed. Distribution itself becomes a line of revenue, not just a cost.

In depth

In depth · 01

Decentralized software has historically asked its users for a sacrifice. Systems that pushed every action through global consensus were slower, clumsier and poorer than their centralized rivals by construction — and no principle survives a ten-second wait to post a message. For open architectures to win, they had to stop being a compromise.

The resolution is a division of labor. Only what genuinely requires incorruptibility — money, settlement, ownership — touches the shared ledger. The applications themselves run in real datacenters with everything co-located and tuned, exactly as the incumbents run theirs, so nothing in the experience betrays the architecture beneath. The difference is confined to who holds what: the user’s data lives in storage the user controls, replicated close to the application for speed, readable to it but never absorbed by it. Walking away means pointing that data at a different provider — not reconstructing a digital life from scratch.

Law is treated with the same seriousness as latency. Each user is served from a site operated by an accountable company in their own country, under their own rules; whatever must differ between countries is configured locally rather than forked from the product. The result reverses the terms the platform era imposed: software that feels global and answers locally, convenience without captivity — and nothing holding the user in place except the product continuing to deserve them.

In depth · 02

What has always been scarce for a founding team is not the ability to build software but everything surrounding it: reaching users, charging them, satisfying a hundred regulators, staffing support at three in the morning. This model lifts all of it off the builder’s desk. The creators’ claim on their work is written into the settlement layer itself — a share of every paid interaction, anywhere on earth, flows to them automatically — which means the code can be given away freely without giving away the living it earns.

The operations land with those equipped to carry them. Local operators deploy the application in their own datacenters, answer to their own regulators, staff their own support — and because they earn from every unit of usage they serve, they promote the product to their subscribers as energetically as a franchisee promotes a brand. A handful of engineers thereby inherits, without hiring anyone, the sales force and operations of a multinational.

Even the financing bends to the same logic. A team can raise money against the usage its product will one day generate, repay it automatically from revenue as it arrives, and emerge unencumbered once the agreed cap is reached — no board seats surrendered, no permanent tax on the product’s future. What disciplines the whole arrangement is the absence of walls: builders who neglect their work can be replaced, and builders who keep deserving their share keep receiving it. Usefulness, continuously renewed, is the only tenure.

In depth · 03

Applications in this environment are reached through addresses that many datacenters announce at once, leaving the network to decide which one answers each request. That decision belongs to the ISP, which controls the routes its subscribers’ traffic takes — and here the decision carries money, because whoever serves the request earns the revenue for operating it.

Both available choices pay. An operator that hosts an application locally keeps the full operating income, serves its subscribers at the lowest possible latency, and never pays to carry the traffic elsewhere. An operator that would rather not run a particular service still holds something of value — the demand itself — and can direct it to partners on agreed terms. Between hosting and steering there is no neutral setting in which the position earns nothing; the operator chooses only in which currency to be paid.

Practiced at scale, this becomes a business of portfolio judgment. Capable operators run catalogues of applications on one shared pool of machines, shifting capacity toward whatever is busy tonight and renting the idle remainder out for background work; over time they specialize around their strengths — one excelling at regulated financial services, another at media, a third at machine-learning workloads on its accelerator fleet. Distribution, a function ISPs have performed unpaid since the network began, matures into their third business — and the one that feeds the other two.

Endgame

ISP-centric Internet

Thousands of federated providers — not one platform — hold the Internet’s liquidity, its data commons and its distribution. Together they form an economic force with stronger network effects than any hyperscaler, and metered pay-as-you-go becomes the default business model of the Internet.

01

Hypercentralization ends — applications can no longer build moats, only better products

Every Internet giant was built on the same two moats: captive distribution and hoarded data. In the endgame neither can be meaningfully built, because the federation always holds more of both — no platform can out-distribute a network that owns the last mile to every user, and no private silo can out-collect a commons aggregated across all of them and open to everyone. What remains is competition on merit: product quality, service quality, price. Positions stop being defensible by structure and must be re-earned in use — Metcalfe’s law stops minting monopolies and goes back to work for the network itself.

In depth
02

Economic activity in both directions feeds a neutral wealth fund — a moat no company can cross

The fund is fed from both sides — households’ bills and buyers of data and compute flow in; per-use payouts to builders and discounts back to households flow out. That circulation makes it less a treasury than a monetary system: the place where the Internet’s economy clears. It decouples the price of access from the cost of service, funds the everyday “free” Internet honestly, and gives the long tail of software a stable income no ad market would ever grant it. And it cannot be out-competed, because neutrality is the one thing a company cannot copy — a private replica of the fund is just another walled garden, and walled gardens repel the very flows that make the pool deep. Like a reserve currency, it wins not because anyone defends it, but because everyone’s economics come to be denominated in it. To beat it you would have to out-pool the planet; to out-pool the planet you would have to be neutral — that is, become it.

In depth
03

Jurisdiction-native decentralization — decentralization that faces the law instead of fleeing it

The old Internet was structurally offshore: global platforms served every country from nowhere, and enforcing local law against them meant a decade in court, if it was possible at all. ESP makes jurisdiction a protocol primitive — settlement clears only between parties matched in the same jurisdiction. A German user is served under German law by a locally accountable operator; compliance stops being fuzzy risk management and becomes a binary, deterministic state. Nation-states regain real oversight of their digital economies — a law passed at home is enforced by the network itself, because breaking it severs revenue rather than triggering a lawsuit. Jurisdictional arbitrage stops being a business model, and the digital economy contributes to the society it operates in instead of extracting value to havens.

In depth

In depth · 01

Every dominant platform of the last generation was constructed from the same three materials: control of the route to users, custody of their identities, and exclusive accumulation of the data their activity produced. The products and brands were the visible surface; those three holdings were the load-bearing structure. The end state described here is simply the condition in which none of the three can be held any longer.

The route to users belongs to the federated network and is priced the same for everyone. Identity stays where it naturally lives — with the provider a household already knows — which vouches for its subscribers toward services without handing over who they are; no application accumulates accounts, because none needs them. And data, flowing into open aggregates that anyone may use, can no longer be hoarded into an advantage: whatever one company learns from the commons, its competitor can learn as well. The raw materials of monopoly are still produced, more abundantly than ever; they simply no longer accumulate anywhere private.

A market stripped of these instruments has one axis left, and it is the one users always wanted: quality. A service that decays or turns against its users can be walked away from, data in hand, and every operator has reasons to make the walking easy — though never free, so that building well still compounds. Scale still matters and networks still concentrate; what they now concentrate is the shared fabric itself, which belongs to no one and therefore threatens no one.

In depth · 02

At the center of this economy sits a single shared fund, and everything passes through it in both directions. Households’ payments flow in, alongside the money paid by buyers of data and computation; payments to applications flow out as usage settles, and discounts flow back to households. It is less a treasury than a circulation — the point at which the Internet’s economy clears — and its reserves blend every kind of revenue the network produces.

The blending is what gives it power. Because connectivity fees, service usage, data income and compute income mingle in one pool, no service has to be priced by its own costs alone: the heavy user of one thing is carried by the light user of another, expensive months are absorbed by quiet ones, and the everyday services that anchor the Internet can be offered generously while strict metering runs invisibly below. It is the mechanism of an insurance pool, applied to an entire economy.

What protects the fund is not its size but its constitution. Any imitation built by a company would have an owner, and an owner is exactly what such a pool cannot survive — no operator will pour its subscribers’ economics into a rival’s coffers. Depth comes only from a neutrality that no company can offer, which is why the fund cannot be competed with, only joined. A position held that way needs no defending; it is simply final.

In depth · 03

The legal signature of the platform era was placelessness. A service could be consumed in one country, operated from a second, incorporated in a third and taxed — if at all — in a fourth; every attempt to govern it slid off toward some other jurisdiction. This was not a failure of regulators but a property of the architecture: where the money and the operation can always move, the law can only ever chase.

The new arrangement removes the escape rather than sharpening the pursuit. Settlement is possible only between parties under the same law: whoever serves a user must be a company established where that user lives, and where that condition fails, the payment simply never clears. Obligations that once required treaties to enforce — consumer protection, taxation, lawful process — now attach automatically to someone within reach. And the operators gain as much as they concede: a local company serving local customers under familiar law is a far safer thing to be than a link in an unaccountable global chain.

For governments, this restores authority without expanding surveillance: rules made at home bind the companies at home, and breaking them interrupts revenue directly — a consequence measured in days rather than in decades of litigation. For the Internet as a whole, it ends the era in which regulatory escape was a business model. What is earned in a society settles there, is taxed there, and helps carry the society it came from.
